# Waterson Writing Guide

## Purpose

This skill defines the **content quality standards** for all Waterson articles on watersonusa.ai. It is referenced by `/publish-article` during article generation (Step 3) to ensure accuracy and consistency.

## 1. Core Product Differentiation

Every article must reflect these facts accurately. Getting any of these wrong damages credibility.

### 1.1 Speed Control — ALL Products

- **Common misconception**: Only hydraulic hinges have speed control
- **Fact**: Waterson uses **two** deceleration technologies:
  - **Hydraulic** (oil-dampened) speed control
  - **Mechanical** speed control
- **Rule**: ALL current Waterson models have speed control. Never imply only certain lines do.

### 1.2 All-Stainless-Steel Construction

- No plastic housings, no aluminum parts
- Key differentiator vs. door closers: more durable, no housing degradation
- **Rule**: Always mention stainless steel construction when comparing to closers or competitors

### 1.3 Certifications & Compliance

| Certification | Detail | Common Error to Avoid |
|---|---|---|
| UL Listed | 1,000,000 cycle test | Do NOT say "UL Certified" — correct term is "UL Listed" |
| NFPA 80 | 3-hour fire rating | Do NOT write 90 minutes (old/incorrect data) |
| ANSI/BHMA A156.17 | Standard US hinge size, direct replacement | Do NOT confuse with A156.1 (butts & hinges) |

### 1.4 8-Foot Door Testing (Exclusive Advantage)

This is Waterson's strongest differentiator. Use it in every relevant article:

- NFPA 80 references A156.17 as the fire door durability test standard
- A156.17 only covers doors up to **7 feet** (3 hinges)
- 8-foot doors (4 hinges) fall in a **regulatory gap** — code says "consult manufacturer"
- **Waterson voluntarily completed UL-standard testing for 8-foot doors**
- **Key message**: "We actually tested to the standard — we didn't just claim compliance"

### 1.5 Self-Closing Hinge vs. Door Closer — Key Advantages

This is a critical comparison that comes up in almost every sales conversation. Always use these points accurately.

#### 1.5.1 No Exposed Body = Full 90° Opening

- Door closers have an exposed body mounted on the door/frame
- When a wall is directly behind the door, the closer body prevents the door from opening fully
- **Waterson self-closing hinges are concealed within the hinge** — no protruding hardware
- This guarantees the door can open to a full **90 degrees** even with a wall behind it
- **ADA relevance**: ADA requires doors to open to 90° when measuring clear width. A door closer that limits the opening angle can cause an ADA violation.

#### 1.5.2 Clean Aesthetics — No Exposed Hardware

- Architects strongly prefer the concealed look — no bulky closer arm or box visible
- The door looks like a normal door with standard hinges
- This is a major selling point for high-end commercial and residential projects

#### 1.5.3 Built-in Door Stop Function

- Waterson hinges can include a **built-in hold-open / door stop** feature
- This eliminates the need for a separate floor-mounted door stop
- **ADA relevance**: A floor door stop behind the door can also prevent 90° opening, creating the same ADA compliance issue
- With Waterson's built-in door stop, both functions (self-closing + door stop) are integrated into the hinge — no additional hardware needed

#### 1.5.4 PVD Custom Finishes

- Standard finishes: stainless steel (satin/polished) and black (powder coat)
- **PVD coating available**: wide range of colors and finishes (gold, bronze, etc.)
- Custom colors available with minimum order quantity (MOQ)
- **Own factory** enables this level of customization — most competitors cannot offer it
- **Rule**: When discussing finishes, emphasize the factory-direct customization capability

### 1.6 Self-Closing Hinge vs. Spring Hinge

#### 1.6.1 Speed Control = ADA Closing Time Compliance

- **ADA requirement**: Door closing time must be **≥ 5 seconds** (from 90° to 12°)
- **Spring hinges CANNOT meet this requirement** — they slam shut with no speed control
- Traditional approach: use a door closer to achieve the 5-second closing time
- **Waterson self-closing hinges solve both problems**: self-closing + speed-controlled closing
- This means: no door closer needed, no exposed hardware, AND ADA-compliant closing time

#### 1.6.2 Durability

- Spring hinges wear out and lose tension over time
- Waterson hinges are tested to 1,000,000 cycles (UL Listed)
- Stainless steel construction vs. typical spring hinge materials

#### 1.6.3 Anti-Slam

- Spring hinges are notorious for door slamming — safety hazard, noise, and door/frame damage
- Waterson's speed control eliminates slamming entirely
- Important for schools, hospitals, senior care facilities, and any high-traffic environment

## 2. Three Target Audiences

Each audience cares about different things. Articles should address **all three** but can emphasize one.

### 2.1 Architects & Specifiers

- Care about: code compliance, spec language, CSI format, finish options
- Want to see: specific NFPA/IBC/ADA section numbers, comparison tables
- Language: technical, precise, standard-referencing
- Example: "Per NFPA 80 Section 6.4.4, self-closing devices on fire doors shall be listed..."

### 2.2 Building Owners & Facility Managers

- Care about: maintenance cost, durability, replacement ease, liability
- Want to see: TCO comparisons, lifecycle data, before/after scenarios
- Language: practical, ROI-focused, problem-solving
- Example: "A standard door closer requires adjustment every 6-12 months. A self-closing hinge requires zero maintenance."

### 2.3 Contractors & Installers

- Care about: installation time, compatibility, hinge count, weight capacity
- Want to see: product-to-application mapping, installation tips
- Language: hands-on, straightforward, tool-oriented
- Example: "K51P drops into any standard 4.5\" x 4.5\" hinge cutout — no additional routing needed."

## 4. Writing Rules

### 4.1 Code & Standard Citations

- **MUST** include the full standard number AND section when citing
  - Good: "NFPA 80 Section 6.4.4"
  - Bad: "according to fire code" or "NFPA regulations"
- After completing the article, **re-verify every citation** against the actual standard
- If you cannot verify a section number, flag it for the user rather than guessing

### 4.2 Data Accuracy

- All test data (cycle counts, fire ratings, weight capacities) must match official specs
- **UL fire rating is 3 hours** — this is confirmed and non-negotiable
- Grade 1 certification: ANSI/BHMA A156.17, 1 million cycle test, force and deflection tolerances
- Pool gate regulations vary by state (FL, CA, etc.) — too complex for definitive statements. Use general language: "consult local building code for pool barrier requirements"

### 4.3 Tone & Style (from watersonusa.com analysis)

The following style guidelines are derived from analyzing published articles on watersonusa.com. Match this voice consistently.

#### Opening — Start with a Relatable Problem

- Always open with a **pain point or scenario** the reader recognizes
- Good: "When winter hits, many homeowners notice something strange: doors that used to glide shut suddenly become stubborn."
- Good: "When you use an asthma inhaler, you rarely think about where it was made."
- Bad: "This article discusses door hinge specifications." (too dry, no hook)

#### Structure — Problem → Diagnosis → Solution → Product

Every article follows this arc:
1. **Problem**: What's going wrong? (slamming, misalignment, code violation)
2. **Diagnosis**: Why does it happen? (technical explanation, accessible language)
3. **Solution concept**: What solves it in general? (speed control, stainless steel, etc.)
4. **Waterson product**: How our specific product delivers that solution

This is **education before selling** — establish expertise through technical depth, then naturally pivot to product features.

#### Voice — Professional yet Accessible

- **Conversational B2B tone** — not academic, not casual
- Use "you/we" language to engage readers directly
- Balance technical specs with plain explanations
  - Good: "Oil-controlled dynamic braking responds effectively to wind gusts"
  - Bad: "The viscous fluid dampening coefficient provides adequate deceleration"
- Short paragraphs (2-4 sentences max), generous use of bullet points and subheadings
- Mix simple declarative sentences with compound sentences that embed explanations
  - Example: "Mechanical self-close hinges have no fluids or seals to wear down, which is why they last significantly longer."

#### Evidence — Ground Claims with Real-World Proof

- Use **real customer scenarios** or case studies with specific details (dimensions, locations, materials)
  - Example: "A residential project in New Jersey: 4.5′ H × 4.5′ W × 3″ thick gates, 100 lbs each"
- Include **customer quotes** when available to humanize technical problems
  - Example: "We had a safety incident where someone smashed their hand"
- Name real installations or partners when permitted (e.g., "KBI Biopharma, Xencor Lab")
- Cite standards naturally within sentences, not as footnotes
  - Good: "ADA Standard 404.2.3 states: Clear openings shall be measured with the door open 90 degrees."

#### Comparison Framing — Let the Reader Self-Select

- When comparing product types (mechanical vs. hydraulic, hinge vs. closer), present both fairly
- Frame differences around **use-case fit**, not "good vs. bad"
  - Good: "Mechanical suits high-traffic; hydraulic suits breezy environments"
  - Bad: "Hydraulic is clearly better than mechanical"
- Use **comparison tables** with concrete specs (warranty years, angle range, temperature tolerance)
- Let the reader identify which solution matches their situation

#### Persuasion — Subtle, Not Salesy

- Present Waterson as a **knowledgeable industry resource**, not just a vendor
- Use specific data points instead of vague claims
  - Good: "Tested to 1,000,000 cycles per ANSI/BHMA A156.17"
  - Bad: "Built to last" or "industry-leading durability"
- When a customer concern arises, **reframe rather than refuse**
  - Example: Instead of "Don't disassemble our hinges", explain why factory custom finishing is superior to DIY
- Educational approach: explain WHY a code exists, not just WHAT it says
- CTA should flow naturally from the educational content — not feel like a hard pivot to sales

### 4.4 Competitor Comparisons

- **Objective and fact-based** — never disparage competitors
- Use real, verifiable data points for comparison
- Focus on Waterson's unique strengths (stainless steel, dual speed control, 8ft testing)
- Name competitors only when making a specific, factual comparison
  - Good: "Unlike traditional overhead closers, self-closing hinges eliminate exposed hydraulic arms that can be vandalized"
  - Bad: "Brand X products are inferior"

### 4.5 Explaining Regulations

- Building codes are dry — always pair with **real-world examples**
- Search for relevant case studies or common scenarios to illustrate the point
- Use callout boxes (`.warning-box`, `.tip-box`) for critical compliance points
- When regulations are ambiguous or jurisdiction-specific, say so explicitly

### 4.6 Things to NEVER Do

1. **Never fabricate test data** — if you don't have the number, ask
2. **Never write "UL Certified"** — the correct term is "UL Listed"
3. **Never state the fire rating as 90 minutes** — it is 3 hours
4. **Never use personal names** for sales reps — use "your local rep" or "our sales team"
5. **Never make definitive pool code statements** — regulations vary by jurisdiction
6. **Never skip the 8-foot door advantage** when it's relevant to the topic
7. **Never imply only hydraulic models have speed control** — all models do
